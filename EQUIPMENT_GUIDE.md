## Black & White Masters

### 1. Ansel Adams (1902-1984)
**Equipment**: 8x10 view camera, B&W film with zone system, f/64 aperture
**Why**: Extreme detail and tonal range for dramatic landscapes
**Signature**: Zone system for perfect exposure control

### 2. Henri Cartier-Bresson (1908-2004)
**Equipment**: Leica rangefinder 35mm or 50mm lens, B&W film (HP5/Tri-X), natural light only
**Why**: Compact, quiet, fast for decisive moment street photography
**Signature**: "The camera is an extension of my eye"

### 3. Diane Arbus (1923-1971)
**Equipment**: Rolleiflex twin-lens reflex 6x6, Tri-X 400, on-camera flash
**Why**: Square format, waist-level viewing for disarming subjects
**Signature**: Direct flash creating psychological intensity

### 4. Irving Penn (1917-2009)
**Equipment**: 8x10 view camera, controlled studio lighting, platinum prints
**Why**: Maximum detail for commercial/editorial perfection
**Signature**: White background corner portraits

### 5. Richard Avedon (1923-2004)
**Equipment**: Deardorff 8x10 view camera, strobe lighting, stark white seamless
**Why**: Unforgiving detail revealing psychological truth
**Signature**: "In the American West" - 8x10 white background portraits

### 6. Annie Leibovitz (1949-)
**Equipment**: Mamiya RZ67 medium format, natural and studio lighting mixed, color and B&W
**Why**: Portable enough for location, quality for magazine covers
**Signature**: Environmental celebrity portraits with narrative

### 7. Robert Mapplethorpe (1946-1989)
**Equipment**: Hasselblad 500C/M medium format, studio lighting, platinum and silver gelatin prints
**Why**: Perfect technical quality for formalist compositions
**Signature**: Classical beauty meets controversial subjects

### 8. SebastiÃ£o Salgado (1944-)
**Equipment**: 35mm and medium format, B&W film, natural light, long-term projects
**Why**: Portable for extended global documentary work
**Signature**: Epic humanitarian photography

### 9. Nan Goldin (1953-)
**Equipment**: 35mm point-and-shoot or SLR, Kodachrome slides (early), Cibachrome prints, on-camera flash
**Why**: Snapshot immediacy for intimate diary photography
**Signature**: "The Ballad of Sexual Dependency" - raw authenticity

### 10. Josef Koudelka (1938-)
**Equipment**: Leica 35mm with 25mm lens (early), later panoramic cameras, B&W film (Tri-X), natural light
**Why**: Wide angle for dramatic perspective, panoramic for landscapes
**Signature**: "Gypsies" and "Exiles" - poetic witnessing

### 11. Mary Ellen Mark (1940-2015)
**Equipment**: 35mm Leica (early), later medium format, Tri-X 400, available light and minimal flash
**Why**: Intimate access to marginalized communities
**Signature**: Empathetic humanist documentary

## Color Photography Masters

### 12. William Eggleston (1939-)
**Equipment**: 35mm Leica, later medium format, Kodachrome slides, dye-transfer printing
**Why**: Dye-transfer for saturated color, elevated mundane subjects
**Signature**: "Democratic" photography - color as subject

### 13. Stephen Shore (1947-)
**Equipment**: 4x5 and 8x10 view cameras, color negative film, C-prints, tripod-based
**Why**: Large format for maximum detail of American vernacular
**Signature**: Deadpan color landscapes and banal beauty

### 14. Saul Leiter (1923-2013)
**Equipment**: 35mm Leica, Kodachrome slides, natural light, shot through windows/rain
**Why**: Spontaneity for abstract color street photography
**Signature**: Painterly reflections and layers

### 15. Steve McCurry (1950-)
**Equipment**: 35mm Nikon, Kodachrome 64, natural light, extensive travel
**Why**: Portable for global assignments, Kodachrome for vivid color
**Signature**: "Afghan Girl" - saturated storytelling

### 16. Alex Webb (1952-)
**Equipment**: 35mm rangefinder (Leica), wide angle lenses (28mm), Kodachrome, harsh sunlight
**Why**: Layered compositions in tropical light
**Signature**: Multiple planes of focus, complex color

## Documentary & Photojournalism

### 22. Dorothea Lange (1895-1965)
**Equipment**: 4x5 Graflex, later Rolleiflex, B&W film, natural light, FSA
**Why**: Portable large format for Depression-era documentation
**Signature**: "Migrant Mother" - empathetic observation

### 23. Walker Evans (1903-1975)
**Equipment**: 8x10 view camera, 4x5 Deardorff, B&W film, available light, tripod
**Why**: Precise documentation of American vernacular
**Signature**: FSA straight photography, "Let Us Now Praise Famous Men"

**Equipment**: 35mm Leica and Nikon, available light and flash, B&W, LIFE magazine

**Signature**: "Minamata" - dramatic lighting, ethical journalism

### 25. James Nachtwey (1948-)
**Equipment**: 35mm Canon and Nikon, B&W and color, available light, conflict zones
**Why**: Durability and speed for war photography
**Signature**: Witness to conflict, humanitarian concern

### 26. Robert Capa (1913-1954)
**Equipment**: 35mm Contax and Leica, B&W film, handheld combat photography
**Why**: Speed and portability in war zones
**Signature**: "If your pictures aren't good enough, you're not close enough"

### Equipment Determines Style:
- **Ricoh GR** (Moriyama) = Raw street spontaneity
- **8x10 view camera** (Adams, Avedon) = Maximum detail and control

- **Kodachrome** (Eggleston, McCurry) = Color as art form
- **Dye-transfer printing** (Eggleston) = Ultimate color control
- **Lightboxes** (Jeff Wall) = Photography as illuminated art
- **Digital** (Gursky) = Massive scale and post-production
- **Instant cameras** (Goldin) = Diary immediacy

### Limitations Created Signatures:
- **Pushed Tri-X** (Moriyama, Gilden) = Grain as aesthetic
- **Available light only** (Cartier-Bresson) = Natural moments
- **Self-timer** (Sherman) = Complete control for self-portraits
- **Car-based** (Hido) = Nocturnal isolation viewpoint
